Where are Australians Born?

Country of Birth of Australians

Please note that the above graph's x-axis does NOT begin at 0.

As expected, the majority of Australians are born in Australia. Generally, many Australians are born in England which makes sense due to Australia being colonised by the British in 1788. A lot of Australians are also born in New Zealand due to the geographically close proxmimity of the two countries.

When this data was first collected in 1996, most Australians who were born overseas were born in Europe. England, Italy, Greece and Germany are among popular countries of birth. Over time, less Australians born overseas are from Europe. More and more Australians are born in all other continents of the world. Of the continents, it appears the more Australians are born in Asia with 655,760 and 845,800 Australians being born in China and India respectively.

What is also evident is that the proportion of Australians who were born in Australia is declining. While there was a slight increase in 2021, there is a general declining trend, likely attributable to increasing amounts of overseas migration into Australia.

Where do Australians Live?

2023 State Population Density (Persons/km2)

Australia is most densely populated in the Australian Capital Territory (ACT), this is mostly due to the territory (197.9 persons per square kilometre) being small in area when compared to the rest of the states and territories. Victoria (VIC) and New South Wales (NSW) are the next most densely populated states with population densities of 30 and 10.4 persons per square kilometre. The least densely populated state/territory is the Northern Territory with a population density of 0.2 persons per square kilometre.

Australians by State and Territory

Births and Deaths by State/Territory

All states and territories in Australia have faced similar declining trends in births between 2018 and 2023. The only exception to this is in 2021 where most states saw a spike in births. The increase was not as pronounced in Tasmania, Australian Capital Territory and the Northern Territory, however, this is attributable to these states and territories smaller sizes. The spike in births is perhaps due to COVID-19 and lockdowns that had occurred in 2020.

Deaths have followed a general increasing trend across all states and territories. There was a sharp increase in most states in 2022 which again is perhaps explained by the aftermath of COVID-19.

Net Interstate Migration by State/Territory

Use the legend to highlight a state!

Net interstate migration is the departures from a particular state deducted from the total arrivals into that state.

Queensland (QLD) has always had a positive net interstate migration. This means that there has always been more people migrating into Queensland from other states than people migrating out of Queensland. Contrastingly, New South Wales (NSW) has always had a negative new interstate migration. QLD and NSW net interstate migration tend to mirror each other - i.e when net interstate migration is highly positive in QLD, it is highly negative in NSW. From this, it is likely that many residents from NSW are migrating to QLD as the states do neighbor one another.

The Australian Capital Territory, Northern Territory and South Australia have all experienced relatively smooth net interstate migration with mininmal large fluctuations. In Western Australia, it is also relatively smooth except for the drop in 2015-2020, then spike up in 2021-2022. As for Victoria major downfalls are observable in 1994-1995 and 2021. As for 2021, the downfall is most likely attributable to the ongoing lockdowns in Victoria due to COVID-19.

Age and Sex of Australians (2021 Census)

Age and Sex Distribution of Australians

Australia has a relatively young population with the majority of Australians in the 25-29, 30-34 or 35-39 age groups Furthermore, more Australians fall in the 0-4 and 5-9 year age groups than the 80-84 and 85 years and older age groups. However, as there are more Australians in the previously mentioned age groups, this is perhaps indicative that Australians are having less children.

In terms of male and female distribution between the age groups, up to the 25-29 year age group, there are more males than females. For all other age groups, there are more females than males. This perhaps tells us that females may live longer than males based on this graph.

The Future Population

Projected Australian Population

Please note that the above graph's x-axis does NOT begin at 0. Use the legend to highlight a scenario!

Australia's population has gradually grown since the beginning of the 2000s. The only exception to this was between 2020 and 2021. As mentioned previously, this was most likely due to COVID-19. Nevertheless, following this dip, in 2022 and 2023 Australia's population continued to grow at a similar rate as in previous years.

As shown, there are three scenarios for Australia's projected future population. The high scenario assumes that Australia's population will continue to grow at a similar rate as it has in the past 20 years with Australia's population in 2071 predicted to be 45.89 million people (increase of just over 19 million). In the medium scenario the population initally grows at the same rate as in the past 20 years but then gradually slows. In this scenario, Australia's population is predicted to be 39.19 million people (increase of under 13 million from 2023). In the low scenario Australia's population growth decreases gradually over time and looks to plateau. In 2071, Australia's population is predicted to be 34.34 million (increase of under 8 million from 2023).

How much Australia's population grows will largely depend on migration rates particularly with conflicts occurring around the world. Furthermore, it will also depend on any health events such as COVID-19 as well as cultural changes regarding families. Over time, people have had less children, particularly in developed countries such as Australia, as such, it will be interesting to see whether fertility rates continue to decrease or will rise again in future.
